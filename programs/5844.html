1Academic FreedomHCPL40203
Winter School - Franklin cohort (2016-2017)
Connected broadening module: Winter Schools are not connected to a specific broadening module.
Destination: Groningen and Amsterdam
Period: February 2019 (two weekends)
Number of places: 22
Atelier in: semester 2 (2018-2019)
Students contribution: between € 50,- and € 60,-
Participation in the atelier from abroad: not possible.

Short description:
“Academic freedom is the freedom of teachers and students to teach, study, and pursue knowledge and research without unreasonable interference or restriction from law, institutional regulations, or public pressure. Its basic elements include the freedom of teachers to inquire into any subject that evokes their intellectual concern; to present their findings to their students, colleagues, and others; to publish their data and conclusions without control or censorship; and to teach in the manner they consider professionally appropriate. For students, the basic elements include the freedom to study subjects that concern them and to form conclusions for themselves and express their opinions.”

Academic Freedom is important for the teachers and students but also for the benefits to society. Society is best served when the educational process leads to the advancement of knowledge, and knowledge is best advanced when inquiry is free from restraints. But what about Academic Freedom in countries without democratic traditions? What about Academic Freedom in modern society, when scholars may be influenced for reasons such as money? What about Academic Freedom and the freedom of expression and publication? What about highly educated student fugitives? What are the threats to Academic Freedom?

The Winter School on Academic Freedom focuses on the three main issues of concern:
• Imprisoned scholars/scholars at risk;
• Ethical questions in modern society;
• Freedom of expression and publication.
The Winter School intends to deepen insights in the complexity of the Academic Freedom related issues mentioned above. The Winter School takes a multidisciplinary approach to the theme, intertwining philosophical, legal, economic, psychological and sociological perspectives to the issues. These issues will be addressed by a mixture of lectures, workshops and discussion sessions. The Winter School will provide all participants with a thought-provoking view on Academic Freedom and all matters involved with it. Participants will have the opportunity to work on case studies and identify action points to effectively contribute to enhancing Academic Freedom. Together with their team and coach, participants will choose a project in order to solve a specific problem and prepare an action plans in order to actively contribute to enhancing Academic Freedom.

3Constructing a PolityHCPL40161
Summer School
Connected broadening module: Constructing a polity - Five features of transitional democracies
Destination: Krakow and Warsaw (Poland)
Period: from 16-22 July 2018
Number of places: 22
Atelier in: semester 2 (2018-2019)
Students' contribution: between € 150,- and € 180,-
Participation in the atelier from abroad: for 5 students
NB: the kick-off meeting for this Summer School will take place on July 9th, 2018 from 11.00-13.00h.

Short description:
Government is an, if not the most, important structural mechanism for (political) societies. Over the last three or four centuries, the Western part of the world has adopted a tradition in which the development of societies is largely dependent on political institutions. It has been argued that this tradition inevitably leads to liberal democracy as the dominant model. Right across historical and political developments, a set of dilemmas or challenges keeps popping up. These dilemmas are topics of constitutional law and they are characteristic of most, if not all democratic societies. Differences of an economical, sociological and political nature determined the way in which these challenges have been faced. In spite of these differences, there is commonality to the political responses to these dilemmas and challenges.

An important question for today is whether the choices for constructing a political society that were made in the past are sustainable for the days to come. Political authority is, due to several developments, becoming less and less self-evident. On a micro level, communities have chosen alternative mechanisms to organize and arrange themselves. On a macro level, ongoing globalization and especially Europeanization puts the statehood of the Member States in a whole new perspective. Moreover, the European Union itself is continuously faced with dilemmas and challenges that its Member States have responded to, earlier on.

The broadening module “Constructing a polity - five features of transitional democracies” sets out to discuss the aforementioned dilemmas and the traditional responses thereto. During the Summer School, these issues will be explored by visiting a state that has undergone several transitions, in all of which the dilemmas and challenges became apparent: Poland. In medieval times, the Polish empire was the largest in Europe. In 1791, the Polish Commonwealth enacted the first modern Constitution on the European continent. Only a few years later, however, the so-called Polish partitions led to the complete dissolution of the Polish Commonwealth as an independent state. It was only after the First World War that Poland reappeared as a sovereign state. This was not the end of the Polish constitutional drama, however. The country disappeared from the map in the Second World War and returned afterwards – but only as a satellite of the Soviet Union. After the fall of communism, and especially after the accession to the European Union in 2004, the Polish constitutional structure underwent yet another transition that gave cause to dilemmas old and new. During the summer school we will study the way in which Poland has dealt with, and keeps dealing with these dilemma's. This enquiry could not be more current, as the Polish struggle between democracy, politics and constitutional law is the topic of many a news item today.

4Diversity, Inclusion and PluralismHCPL40172
Summer School
Connected broadening module: Diversity and inclusion in the urban context
Destination: Vancouver, Canada
Period: 8 days during the period of July 26th (day of arrival) and August 4th 2018 (day of departure)
Number of places: 22
Atelier in: semester 1 & 2 (2018-2019) depending on student activities
Students contribution: about € 550,-
Participation in the atelier from abroad: students that spent a semester abroad can participate in the atelier.

Short description:

This summer school is a direct extension from the broadening module and the research conducted in Groningen. Whilst on location, students will explore dimensions of diversity and inclusion in the (third) most liveable city of the world, i.e Vancouver.

Our key aim will be to provide a multi-dimensional and critical perspective on the inclusiveness of the city for marginalized groups. In so doing, we will compare and contrast the findings from our Groningen research. Preparation for the summer school includes reading literature on issues of city planning, diversity, inclusion and marginalization in Vancouver, and writing a research plan for the group project.

After a city walk led by the director of the City Program at Simon Fraser University our week will include input on various aspects of the topic by a variety of researchers based at Simon Fraser University and the University of British Columbia.

Other important parts of the summer school will be an individual assignment (tba)and a group project. The group project consist of fieldwork that will be carried out to gain perspectives of people living in Vancouver, and students will therefore need to approach people and organisations for interviews. Students will also collect visual data. Preliminary results of the student projects will be presented on the last day during a hike in the forest.

The summer school will include an assessment of the individual assignments, the research plan, the project presentations, and the development of a public product (Petrus Camper Festival).

5Earthquakes in GroningenHCPL40208
Summer School - Franklin cohort (2016-2017)
Connected broadening module: Earthquakes in Groningen
Destination: the province of Groningen
Period: 5 days during the week of 2-8 July 2018
Number of places: 22
Atelier in: semester 1 & 2 (2018-2019)
Students' contribution: between € 150,- and € 170,-
Participation in the atelier from abroad: not possible

Short description:
Since 1963, gas extraction is taking place in the Northern Province of Groningen in the Netherlands. For a long time, the gas extraction generated a lot of revenues for the Dutch state. The population in Groningen was proud of the gas extraction and that it contributed to the growing wealth of the Netherlands. However, the first negative consequences of the gas extraction started to be revealed since the 1980s when the first signs of ground movement were observed. Whereas the relationship between the gas extraction and ground movement was denied for a many years, the influence of the gas extraction on the soil was acknowledged when the first signs of soil subsidence came to the fore. In the years that followed, earthquakes were observed which were also a result of the decreasing pressure in the earth due to the gas extraction. From the 1990s until 2012, the earthquakes were of a low magnitude and caused mainly problems with regard to damage and cracks in walls of houses and buildings. However, this changed in August 2012 when an earthquake hit near the village of Huizinge. This earthquake, with a magnitude of 3.6 on the Richter scale, marks a big change in the earthquake and gas extraction issues, as this was no longer limited to damage issues, but extended to safety matters of the population.

In order to deal in an adequate way with the problems, several institutions are installed to coordinate the actions of the different local tiers of government and to conduct damage assessments. Yet, the Dutch state is very much interwoven with the private company that extracts the gas from the soil. These public-private sector interrelationships make the governance system for dealing with and solving the issues highly complex. These characteristics of the earthquakes have a big influence on the social acceptance and impact, the mental wellbeing of the population, the decreasing housing and real estate values of the properties in the area, and governance approach to deal with the earthquake issues.

In this Summer School we dive deeper into these complex problems. Students will actively engage with the issues by a simulation game. The students will be split up in groups of different stakeholders in this issue. For example, one group represents the private gas extraction company, one group represents the national government, one group represents affected citizens, one group represents the media, etc. The general assignment for the week is: to work together to find a fitting solution for the issues in Groningen.

To reach a better understanding of the interests of the different parties involved, we will organize several activities in the Province of Groningen. For example, we will visit damaged houses and monuments. We will talk to activists, public officials, representatives of the government and civil society organizations working on the gas extraction issues. At the end of the week, students will have a good comprehension of the core of the problems and be able to take in a nuanced standpoint on the matter.

6Energy Law in a Changing WorldHCPL40206
Summer School
Connected broadening module: Energy Law in a Changing World: Technological and Legal Innovations
Destination: Washington D.C., U.S.A and Groningen, the Netherlands
Period: August 2018 (Washington D.C.) and approx. April/May 2019 (Groningen)
Number of places: 10
Atelier in: Semester 2 (2018-2019)
Students contribution: about € 550,-
Participation in the atelier from abroad: for a few students

Short description:
The summer school offers a programme of joint research by students from the Honours College of the RUG and students from the George Washington Law School in the US. The programme is open for law students and a limited number of students from other faculties.

The research is titled ‘Energy Law in a Changing World: Technological and Legal Innovations’. Realizing the global commitment to reduce carbon emissions, which has recently been reaffirmed in the Paris Agreement, requires a vast amount of innovative technologies and solutions to be integrated into the global energy system. The integration of such technologies and solutions also requires their acknowledgement in legislation, assigning roles and responsibilities to all parties involved. Issues to be discussed involve innovative topics such as prosumers and smart grids, hydrogen, biogas, re-use of installations, e-storage.

The research focuses on these developments from a EU (Dutch) and US perspective and will be organized in the following way:
• In the period February – May the students will be involved in a teaching program (broadening module), which aims at familiarizing the students with the legal issues involved.
• Each student will be required to examine in detail one of the above issues in order to be prepared for the RUG summer school.
• In August 2018 the RUG and GW students will meet in Washington. During this week the students will be divided in groups consisting of GW and RUG students. Each group will focus on one of the above topics and present the preliminary outcome of their research at the end of that week.
• Thereafter the Dutch and US students will turn their papers into an article. These papers will be finalized and presented in Groningen during Spring 2019.
• Published articles will be presented at the Petrus Camper Festival in June 2019.

7Frontiers of ScienceHCPL40191
Summer School
Connected broadening module: From Small to Large to Life (semester 1 or 2)
Destination: Geneva, Switzerland (CERN)
Period: 9-14 July 2018
Number of places: 22
Atelier in: semester 1 & 2 (2018-2019)
Students’ contribution: between € 200,‐ and € 220,‐
Participation in the atelier from abroad: for 4-5 students

Short description:

The Summer School is a follow-up on the honours module ‘From small to large to life’. This course consists of a discovery journey through the physics of the very small and the very large, from elementary particles and strings, through the molecular world at nanoscale dimensions, and through the world of genes and biodiversity to galaxies and the universe.

The Summer School consists of five topical days with interactive lectures, discussions, workshops and evening presentations in Geneva (Switzerland). Forefront scientific research topics and their ethical and societal impact will be discussed.

In Geneva we will visit CERN (Conseil Européen pour la Recherche Nucléaire).
At CERN we will learn about fundamental research and see inside the world’s largest particle physics laboratory. You will hear, first hand, about exciting physics and related disciplines, and you will see the enormous scale of the enterprise. Unless you see it you will not fully appreciate the magnitude of the task and the achievement.

Particle Physics is about answering some of the ‘big questions’ in science. What are the basic building blocks of matter? How did the Universe begin? CERN is one of the places where we may find some of the answers. During our visit we might include one of the LHC experiment controls rooms, the magnet assembly hall or even a chance to see one of the experiments itself, 100m underground (it depends on what is available at the time of your visit). We may also be able to visit the antimatter factory, the computer center which handles data from the LHC or the Proton Synchrotron at the heart of the CERN accelerator complex.

Next to CERN we will also visit one or two other international organizations based in Geneva.

Happiness, broadly understood, is universally desired. But how can we, both as individuals and as society, reach a state of happiness? Short description:

‘Post-truth’ was chosen by the Oxford Dictionary as the word of the year 2016 – the year in which president-elect Trump started to produce ‘alternative facts’. He, and others before him, are accused of ‘fact-free politics’. Supposed remedy: let the facts speak again. See the popularity of fact-checkers. But is it that simple? Do scientific, objective facts speak for themselves – separated from political, subjective values? Or are science and politics much more intertwined than this traditional answer suggests? See discussions about, e.g., climate change, medical treatments, or economic models. Where science ends and politics begins? Should policy decisions be based on scientific knowledge or on political ideals? Or, may be, on a mix of both? And if so, what are the implications for the relation between scientific experts, politicians and citizens? For the relation between science and democracy? Who is allowed to talk when about what? And what about the role of intermediaries between science and politics: applied researchers, governmental officials, policy advisors, journalists, spin-docters, consultants, communication officers, etc.?

To get acquainted in real practice with the complex relations between science and politics, we go to the place where – in the Netherlands – both meet each other par excellence: The Hague. Here we find not only the national parliament (including Nieuwspoort – the centre where journalists and politicians meet each other ), but also several policy advise institutes – like the Scientific Council for Government Policy, the Health Council, the Education Council, the National Institute for Public Health and the Environment, the Netherlands Bureau for Economic Policy Analysis, etc. We will visit some of these institutions and their representatives – including (members of) the parliament.

In the atelier students will select and analyze case-studies, exemplary for the entanglement of science and politics. The cases can be Dutch, but, especially for non-Dutch participants, also from abroad – making an international comparison possible.

Short description:

The Waddensea with its beautiful islands are a world heritage. Whereas many people know the islands, only those who sail there really experience the magic of the largest sea that falls dry twice a day.

The Dutch Waddenislands aim for energy independency from the mainland by 2020. Whereas different technological options are being considered to realize this, one of the main hurdles is the support of the population and tourists for these plans. How to make people enthusiastic and willing to contribute to solar power farms, windmills, and perhaps electrical vehicles and smart grid technology? Technology is often well developed, and economically many options are viable, yet it is often hard to reach a social tipping point where people switch from waiting to action. In the Summer School we will focus on this question, which is relevant for the energy transition in general.

The Summer School will take place from August 26th (depart around 18:00) – August 31st (arrival around 17:00), and will be situated on the clipper Willem Jacob (and if necessary a second ship). On this beautiful, fast and green key certified clipper built in 1889, we will sail from Harlingen to Terschelling, Ameland, and Schiermonnikoog to arrive at Lauwersoog. On the islands, and at the dry-falling Waddenseas, we will discuss sustainability with a variety of interesting people from the islands, not only emphasizing a technical view, but also interesting narratives and artistic approaches that contribute to the shaping of a sustainable future. This is expected to give a much deeper insight in the complexities and opportunities of growing towards a sustainable society in general. Also, it will help us to define a research question for the connected Atelier afterwards, in which answers will be sought for that are relevant for the Islands and the world in general in reaching for sustainability. Important is that we start thinking about long-term perspectives, something that will be emphasized by my story about the history of the region, and the technical and social developments that happened during the last 50 years.

The sailing will serve as a natural metaphor for managing complex systems. Whereas we have a very experienced captain, shipmate and not to forget a great cook, you are expected to work aboard as well. You will learn about maneuvering such a large ship and navigating it though the wonderful tidal zone of the Waddensea, which is World Heritage. You will experience a walk on the sea bottom, swim in the middle of nature and encounter many seals. One day we’ll sleep in the midst of the sea on anchor, the next day we stay in the cozy harbor of Terschelling, and obviously we will have a dance party at the Tox bar on Schiermonnikoog.

Being an interdisciplinary scientists myself, originating from social psychology, but very much into complexity science, and also fond of natural sciences, I hope to share a lot of my knowledge, experiences and doubts with you in vivid discussions. Also I’m sailing a lot on the Waddensea myself, and am therefore very familiar with the history and interesting aspects of the different islands.

Living on this ship together for a week is a unique experience, and I’m sure we will discuss a lot about science in general, life and nature. I also hope to inspire you with out-of-the-box thinking, interdisciplinary thinking and making science fun.
