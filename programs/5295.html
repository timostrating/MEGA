Opmerkingen

1. Students have the option to replace the second semester Research Seminar with an MA Internship (10 ECTS), which is to be carried out during the second semester, in principle in the U.S. or the Americas.
2. Pending availability of exchange places, eligible students have the option to do additional course work during the second semester at one of the institutions in the Americas with which the degree program has a formal exchange agreement. Students doing course work in America will in principle complete the equivalent of 3 research seminars at 400 level or higher.

1Leaders and FollowersLAX039M10
This seminar considers how leadership worked in early America and how it relates to leadership today. It addresses problems in political culture such as: how subjects and officials negotiated authority in diverse colonial and postcolonial settings; how and why certain political communities achieved stability while others did not; and to what extent deference and disobedience characterized settlements in the New World. But it also looks beyond traditional political questions to examine how leadership was expressed (and contested) in other arenas, including indigenous communities, radical religious associations, households, and workplaces ranging from plantations to pirate ships. Ultimately, the course aims to historicize leadership in different contexts and eras but also to draw out connections and parallels across those various settings. Readings will be drawn from primary sources (ca. 1600-1800) and related secondary materials in history, political science, cultural studies, and business and management studies.

4Politics & Culture 21st Century USLAX041M10
Inspired by Hannah Arendt's 1972 essay collection, Crises of the Republic, this course examines the ways in which the still-young 21st century might or might not be usefully characterized as one in which the United States has found itself afflicted by a succession of crises, from the national security crisis ushered in by the attacks of 9/11 to the global financial crisis that began in 2007-08 and the ongoing "constitutional crisis" that some have argued the Trump presidency has precipitated.

We will study specific 21st century crises in detail, but also explore and seek to problematize the history and theory of crisis as a concept, while investigating the ways in which different forms of narrative culture, including novels, films and TV series, have represented various "crises" of the 21st century U.S.

5Religion in the BorderlandsLAX036M10
This seminar centers religions in the U.S.-Mexico borderlands to consider the cultural, historical, and political position of this region in relationship to governing bodies, cultural groups and religious peoples and organizations. Religion challenges and changes the meanings and uses of democracy in frontier areas, where authority is distant and local power is often centralized in, around, or in contradistinction to the church. The focus of the course is religion in the U.S.-Mexico borderlands in the past 150 years. However, we will be attentive to the legacies of religious institutions, practices, and conflicts that frame this era. This liminal space served and serves to delineate the limits of colonial and contemporary political bodies. Religions and the religious have also been shaped by the socio-political dimensions of this national, international and transnational landscape. This seminar considers how this region is understood in historical, literary, and popular cultural contexts and how its Americanness is perceived and shaped by religious, cultural, racial, discursive, and political forces. The haunting of the borderlands by indigenous and Spanish religious and cultural formations marks the distinctive character of the region. The ethical challenges and choices that are revealed in this space showcase the making of the American nation and its relations to Mexico and Latin America in the past century and a half.

6Rhetoric and Identity in AmericaLAX040M10
This course starts from the premise that American culture is fundamentally rhetorical. The United States was conceived in speech and speech has been a favorite medium of those—ranging from Frederick Douglas to Jerry Falwell to Lady Gaga—seeking to rally support for their vision for America. In this course we will study the medium of the speech as a defining feature and force of change in American political culture, past and present. In particular, we will focus on how speakers (re)defined and challenged the narrative of what it means to be American. We will study the roots of American speech culture in the republican notion of appealing to the people, as well as the threat that (populist) rhetoric is considered to be to democracy. Students will familiarize themselves with rhetorical theory and speech criticism and reflect on and join current issues debated in the field. We will explore the full breadth and variety of the speech genre—from State of the Union to Oscar Acceptance—and the increasingly blurring lines between them (celebrity politics, etc.)
