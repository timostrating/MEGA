2Aardbevingen in GroningenHCRUB0605
Since 1963, gas extraction is taking place in the Northern Province of Groningen in the Netherlands. For a long time, the gas extraction generated a lot of revenues for the Dutch state. The population in Groningen was proud of the gas extraction and that it contributed to the growing wealth of the Netherlands. However, the first negative consequences of the gas extraction started to be revealed since the 1980s when the first signs of ground movement were observed. Whereas the relationship between the gas extraction and ground movement was denied for a many years, the influence of the gas extraction on the soil was acknowledged when the first signs of soil subsidence came to the fore. In the years that followed, earthquakes were observed which were also a result of the decreasing pressure in the earth due to the gas extraction. From the 1990s untill 2012, the earthquakes were of a low magnitude and caused mainly problems with regard to damage and cracks in walls of houses and buildings. However, this changed in August 2012 when an earthquake hit near the village of Huizinge. This earthquake, with a magnitude of 3.6 on the Richter scale, marks a big change in the earthquake and gas extraction issues, as this was no longer limited to damage issues, but extended to safety matters of the population.

In order to deal in an adequate way with the problems, several institutions are installed to coordinate the actions of the different local tiers of government and to conduct damage assessments. Yet, the Dutch state is very much interwoven with the private company that extracts the gas from the soil. These public-private sector interrelationships make the governance system for dealing with and solving the issues highly complex. These characteristics of the earthquakes have a big influence on the social acceptance and impact, the mental wellbeing of the population, the decreasing housing and real estate values of the properties in the area, and governance approach to deal with the earthquake issues.

In this broadening module, we will delve into the various topics related to the earthquake issues in Groningen. From an interdisciplinary perspective, professors from different expertise areas will share their insights into the earthquake and gas extraction issues. At the end of the course, students are able to understand the situation of the earthquakes and gas extraction in Groningen in an integral way and to reflect on societal, economic and technical complexities inherent to extractive industries.

3Addiction and Addictive BehaviorHCRUB0705
Heroin, crack and cigarettes are addictive. How about cannabinoids? Or cocaine? What is the difference between dependence and addiction? Can you be addicted to behavior? What exactly is addiction to gambling, running or eating? Should Anorexia Nervosa be considered as an addiction to dieting and exercise?

Neuroscientists agree that the dopaminergic reward system in the brain plays an essential role in the development from reward to addiction. But when does pleasure change into compulsive behavior? Or in other words when and why does ‘liking’ turns into ‘wanting?

Many questions are related to the topic of addiction, a serious problem in our Society. Neuroscientist Anton Scheurink (Teacher of the Year 2014) promised to set up a broadening module for Hounours College on this topic.

Anton’s perspective is that simple chemical and neurobiological pathways cannot completely explain all underlying mechanisms involved in addiction to behavior and/or drugs. For example, environmental cues have a major impact on the development of addiction and changing the environment is one of the most effective ways to treat addicts (and to prevent relapse). Therefore, a course on reward and addiction should approach the topic from an interdisciplinary perspective. Pharmacists, laywers, psychiatrists, sociologists, neuroscientists and psychologists will be asked to contribute to this module. Not only from a theoretical perspective but also in real life: Verslavingszorg Noord Nederland has already agreed to participate. This may evoke discussions on how the theoretical background should be translated into socially relevant questions. Warning: you may end with a completely different view on drinking and driving!

4Argumentation and Political DeliberationHCWBB0605
The goal of the broadening module ‘Argumentation and Deliberation’ is to study argumentation in political deliberation. What purposes do political argumentation and criticism serve in political deliberation, by what standards can we evaluate political arguments, and how can the quality of political deliberation be enhanced? We deal with these issues from two points of view. First, from the perspective of deliberation theory, a branch within political philosophy. Deliberation theory starts from the idea that a political decision can only be legitimate if it is the result of a procedure in which citizens have tested the decision, as well as its rivals, by weighing the reasons pro and con in light of the common good. Second, we deal with these issues from the point of view of the philosophy of argument, which provides us with tools for the linguistic analysis and logical evaluation of argumentation and criticism. One recurrent issue is how cooperation and competition should be balanced in order for a political exchange of argumentation and critique to be reasonable. We apply the acquired insights in class when analyzing and evaluating examples of political discussion, and we shall pay ample attention to election debates. After an examination of these philosophical issues, we try and test home-made recommendations for making election debate more genuinely argumentative and deliberative.

7Constructing a polityHCRGB0505
Government is an, if not the most, important structural mechanism for (political) societies. Over the last three or four centuries, the Western part of the world has adopted a tradition in which the development of societies is largely dependent on political institutions. It has been argued that this tradition inevitably leads to liberal democracy as the dominant model. Right across historical and political developments, a set of dilemmas or challenges keeps popping up. These dilemmas are topics of constitutional law and they are characteristic for most, if not all, democratic societies. Differences of an economical, sociological and political nature determined the way in which these challenges have been faced. In spite of these differences, there is commonality to the political responses to these dilemmas and challenges.
An important question today is whether or not the choices for constructing a political society that were made in the past, are sustainable for the days to come. Political authority is, due to several developments, becoming less and less self-evident. On a micro level, communities have chosen alternative mechanisms to organize and arrange themselves. On a macro level, ongoing globalization and especially Europeanization puts the statehood of the Member States in a whole new perspective. Moreover, the European Union itself is continuously faced with dilemmas and challenges that its Member States have responded to, earlier on.
The broadening module “Constructing a polity - five features of transitional democracies” sets out to discuss the aforementioned dilemmas and the traditional responses thereto. During the summer school, these issues will be explored by visiting a state that has undergone several transitions, in all of which the dilemmas and challenges became apparent: Poland. In medieval times, the Polish empire was the largest in Europe. In 1791, the Polish Commonwealth enacted the first modern Constitution on the European continent. Only a few years later, however, the so-called Polish partitions led to the complete dissolution of the Polish Commonwealth as an independent state. It was only after the First World War that Poland reappeared as a sovereign state. This was not the end of the Polish constitutional drama, however. The country disappeared from the map in the Second World War and returned afterwards – but only as a satellite of the Soviet Union. After the fall of communism, and especially after the accession to the European Union in 2004, the Polish constitutional structure underwent yet another transition that gave cause to dilemmas old and new.

The following topics are discussed:
  • Constitutional law as an instrument of organizing a polity
  • Representation
  • Fundamental rights
  • Horizontal separation of powers
  • Vertical separation of powers
  • Supervision and enforcement
  • Europeanisation and constitutional democracies

10Danpaati Project SurinamHCMWB0505
In the deep jungle of Surinam, Danpaati River Lodge supports regional healthcare and sustainable regional developments. The local community exists of 10.000 Marrons. They fled from their slave masters and built their own society. They have their own local language: Sarramacan. Most of the Marrons still live a traditional life with their animistic religion and rituals. Many men and young adults leave those villages and move to Paramaribo or French Guyana. 19From Small to Large to LifeHCWNB0105
A central concept in the beta-sciences is the relevance of scales. Timescales, scales of energy, and scales of size are crucial to understand when dealing with elementary particles, the function and structure of proteins, functional nano-materials or the driving forces behind evolution. In this course we will explore the physical world from small to large to life, thereby covering an enormous range of scales in size, energy and time.
We will start with an overview of these different scales, and explore key examples. Following this overview, we will encounter the quantum world of elementary particles and the four fundamental forces between them. How well do we understand Nature on such short length scales? What are the time and energy scales that govern this world? Then we move up from the elementary particles to the atoms and molecules, and discover how the careful study of these composite particles can be connected to the spectacular evolution of the universe since the Big Bang.
In the second part of this course we will enter a discovery journey through the molecular world at nanoscale dimensions. It starts with the structure of molecules and ‘en route’ one experiences the design and building of a new molecule and the way molecules interact and communicate with each other. We will try to understand how assemblies of tiny atoms result in a fascinating diversity of functions, ranging from a living cell to a computer chip. During our journey, the concepts of chemistry will be illustrated with molecules and materials that dramatically changed our daily life, such as antibiotics and display materials. It will end with a glimpse in the futuristic world of nanorobots.
In the third part we will focus on the meaning of heritability and genes but also on the carrier of inheritable information, DNA, and its structure. Additionally, the mechanism behind the translation of information on the DNA will be briefly discussed. Finally, it will be explained how DNA may be used in the identification of individuals and how genes are involved in diseases.
Mendels laws of inheritance form the basis of the classic genetics. Heritability of traits is also an essential part of Darwin's Theory of Evolution. Around 1920, population genetics and evolutionary theory were integrated in what is now known as Neo-Darwinism. In modern evolutionary biology, we use Neo-Darwinism to explain how organisms can adapt to their environments (micro-evolution) and how new species originate (macro-evolution). In this series, we will also address the evolution of life history strategies, of sex and of humans.

--Opbouw Pick Your Own-talent programma--

Jaar 1 Welcoming Ceremony: gezamenlijke start van het Bachelor Honours programma

Tijdens de openingsceremonie komen alle nieuwe honoursstudenten bij elkaar. Elk jaar wordt er een aansprekende en inspirerende gastspreker uitgenodigd. Hij of zij vertelt over zijn of haar talent, carrière en over de keuzes die hij of zij heeft gemaakt om zijn of haar talenten ten volle te benutten.

Jaar 2 Kick-off: gezamenlijke start van het tweede jaar

Het tweede academische jaar start met de Kick-off. Tijdens deze avond inspireert een talentvolle spreker de tweedejaars honoursstudenten door hen na te laten denken over vragen als ‘wie ben ik, wat kan ik en wat wil ik’ en ‘hoe kan ik dat bereiken?’. Ieder jaar wordt er een andere inspirerende spreker uitgenodigd. Deze avond vindt dit jaar plaats op woensdag 6 september 2017 en is verplicht voor iedere tweedejaars honoursstudent.

Jaar 2 Plenaire avond naar keuze: Academia of Entrepreneurship (keuzegroep E)

Vervolgens wordt de blik gericht op het werkveld. In dit onderdeel oriënteer je je op werken in de wetenschap of op Entrepreneurship (ondernemen met al je talenten in de breedste zin van het woord). Waar zou je na je studie willen werken? Ambieer je een carrière in de wetenschap of weet je al dat je in het bedrijfsleven of de publieke sector aan de slag wilt? Misschien weet je het nog niet. Om je al halverwege je bachelor studie in aanraking te brengen met de Academia en met Entrepreneurship, worden er twee plenaire avonden georganiseerd met een interactief karakter. Tijdens deze twee avonden staat telkens één van de twee thema’s centraal. Deze avonden worden georganiseerd op respectievelijk dinsdag 14 november 2016 en op donderdag 8 februari 2018. Het deelnemen aan één van deze twee avonden is verplicht voor iedere tweedejaars honoursstudent.

Jaar 2 Workshops (keuzegroep F)

In het tweede semester van het tweede jaar ga je zelf aan de slag met jouw potentiële talent. Er zijn verschillende workshops met thema’s zoals ‘passie’, ‘wilskracht’, ‘pick your own time’, ‘omgaan met keuzestress’, ‘sturen of coachen’ en ‘succes en ambitie’. De workshops worden zowel in het Nederlands als in het Engels aangeboden. Het doel van de workshop is dat je kennis maakt met het betreffende onderwerp en hiermee aan de slag gaat. Tijdens de startbijeenkomst van de workshop worden verschillende aspecten geïntroduceerd. Gedurende een periode van één of twee weken neem je jouw talent onder de loep en probeer je de opgedane kennis in de praktijk te brengen. De workshop wordt afgesloten met een slotbijeenkomst, waarin je reflecteert op wat je hebt geleerd. Bovendien kijk je vooruit naar hoe je het thema kunt gebruiken in jouw studiecarrière of zelfs daarna. Iedere workshop bestaat uit twee sessies. De workshops worden in semester II gegeven. Het volgen van één workshop is verplicht voor ieder tweedejaars honoursstudent.

Jaar 3 Workshops (keuzegroep I)

In het derde jaar wordt de laatste stap in het PYO-t programma genomen en sta je gericht stil bij ‘wat wil ik’. Welke carrière ambieer je? Je kunt kiezen uit verschillende thema’s en ontwikkelt een loopbaanprofiel of verkent de arbeidsmarkt in relatie tot jouw ambitie. Ook kun je bijvoorbeeld onderzoeken of je een onderneming op wilt starten. Andere thema’s zijn o.a. 'bouw aan je persoonlijke merk' en 'het leven als experiment'. In het derde jaar van je studie maak je al belangrijke keuzes voor jouw toekomstige carrière. De eerste stappen richting de ontwikkeling van jouw talenten zijn gezet, wat zijn jouw volgende stappen? Welke talenten kun jij in de toekomst aanwenden om al jouw kennis en kunde optimaal in te kunnen zetten ten behoeve van wetenschap, maatschappij of elders. Iedere workshop bestaat uit twee sessies. De workshops worden het gehele derde jaar aangeboden, dus je kunt òf in semester I òf in semester II een workshop volgen. Kijk dus goed naar de planning van bijvoorbeeld een buitenlandverblijf, het schrijven van je scriptie, etc. Het volgen van één workshop is verplicht voor iedere derdejaars honoursstudent.

26Propagating the PapacyHCLEB0505
Around 1200, the Papacy was at the height of its power. There were no other rulers strong enough to dispute its claims of supremacy in both spiritual and secular matters. In the subsequent centuries, however, the Papacy lost much of its prestige and reached its lowest point around 1400, when three persons each claimed to be the only, legitimate pope. A solution was wrought in 1417, when the original situation of the Papacy headed by one pope, residing in Rome, was restored. To illustrate the Papacy’s recovered prestige, a series of large projects involving buildings, sculptures and paintings was initiated. The central question of this course will be: How do these projects express the claims and pretensions of the Papacy and of the individual popes in their struggle for (renewed) recognition as the supreme authority in both spiritual and secular matters? Monuments to be studied include the church of St Peter, the Sistine Chapel and the Stanze of Raphael.

49Social ComplexityHCRUB0205
In most social systems the interactions between individuals give rise to aggregate (collective) phenomena which in turn set the stage for individual behavior. Volatility in financial markets, fashions and hypes in consumer markets, organizational cultures, team processes, spatial organization of (economic) activities, opinion dynamics in society, demonstrations and riots, and social stratification in (animal) societies are just a few issues that confront us with often unpredicted and surprising outcomes. Whereas the aforementioned issues relate to many different fields of application, they share the principle that interactions between individuals (micro level) give rise to the emergence of macro-level phenomena, which in turn set the stage for individual behavior (downward causation or immergence). A micro-macro interaction perspective is therefore required in understanding the underlying processes leading towards such outcomes. This micro-macro perspective is a cornerstone in complex systems science, a paradigm which is embraced by an increasing number of scientists and policy makers from different disciplines as a viable approach in understanding the behavior of complex systems and their management. Whereas the principle of complexity has been identified decades ago as a perspective to study systems involving many interconnected variables (e.g., Weaver, 1948), the rise of computing power and methods such as agent-based simulation provided means to experiment with complex systems. Rather than focusing on classical prediction, complex system approaches focus on unraveling the processes of interaction that give rise to the aggregated phenomena. This also offers a perspective on the management of such complex behaving systems, a challenge for managers and policy-makers in many different fields, such as climate-change, marketing,management, social policy and spatial planning.

The course is aimed at students from different disciplinary backgrounds, and offers them an overview of how the social complexity perspective is being used in studying a variety of topics. 1: Introduction of the principles of social complexity

Het laatste onderdeel van de Petrus Camper Leergang is een onderdeel waarin alle componenten zijn samengebracht en waarin studenten zich actief, in een interdisciplinaire setting, bezighouden met een maatschappelijk thema/vraagstuk. De thema's/vraagstukken waaruit gekozen kan worden vloeien voort uit de verbredende modules die door de faculteiten worden aangeboden.
Iedere faculteit biedt één honoursmodule aan, waarmee het aantal thema's op negen komt. De student kiest voor het thema van één van de twee modules die hij/zij in het tweede jaar heeft gevolgd. Het thema is daarmee voor de student per definitie verbredend, aangezien modules gekozen dienen te worden uit het aanbod van andere faculteiten dan de eigen faculteit.
Dit onderdeel van de PCL begint aan het einde van het tweede jaar met een Summer School (of Winter School in het 3e jaar). Deze dient als startbijeenkomst en introductie op het thema. Vervolgens wordt het thema/vraagstuk door de studenten zelf in een atelier verder geëxploreerd en uitgewerkt, waarna dit onderdeel wordt afgesloten met een presentatie (in de ruimste zin des woords) tijdens het Petrus Camper Festival aan het einde van jaar 3.
De begeleiding van Summer/Winter School en Atelier is door de koppeling aan de verbredende modules verdeeld over de negen faculteiten.

52Sustainable MarketsHCEBB0105
Current consumption patterns are not sustainable – they jeopardize the prosperity and well-being of future generations: fossil fuels are being depleted, fish stocks are decreasing, rain forests are being cut down to make room for agriculture, and climate change threatens prosperity of future generations. Societies are also threatened by over-consumption, with multiple consequences for the individual consumer, such as obesity and addiction. Furthermore, it has been acknowledged that our current (Western) consumption style occurs at the expense of human well-being in non-Western countries and animals. In sum, there is an increasing pledge in society to move to more sustainable consumption behavior, and thus markets.
In this course, we will first discuss the concept of sustainability with the sustainable society index (http://www.ssfindex.com/) as a starting point. We will then identify the main challenges in moving towards a sustainable society, and discuss different aspects of sustainable consumption and sustainable markets, using insights from economics, sociology and psychology. We will on the one hand highlight sustainability from a consumers’ perspective and address topics such as sustainable consumer choices and consumers’ choices in favor or against a more healthy eating pattern. On the other hand, we will also take the perspective of suppliers and producers and discuss the management of complex systems and the transition to sustainable energy markets. We will conclude with an integrated view on “transition management” as a multi disciplinary perspective on how to stimulate a transition towards a sustainable society. In the classes we will have guest lectures from scientists and practitioners from different disciplines.

53Why Nations FailHCRWB0705
This course addresses institutions and how important they are for success, or failure of nations. It also shows that there are no easy solutions. The course starts by introducing the concept of transaction costs and the effect it has on our society. Next, the course builds upon this insight and applies it to different countries in different historical settings to explain failure or success on a nation-wide level.
