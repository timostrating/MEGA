1Ba Thesis English (Linguistics)LET999B10
In this module, students will be supervised while undertaking independent research in preparation for a BA thesis in English linguistics. During the first half of the semester, they will work on setting up their linguistics research project. If and when relevant to the students' projects, there will be classes on linguistic background, qualitative and quantitative methods, statistics, practical research skills, etc. During the second half of the course the students will focus on executing their own research and writing the BA thesis that reports on the research project. The instructor provides supervision and coaching throughout the process.

4Broken Images: Anglophone ModernismLEL018B05
This course will explore literary modernism in English, from some of its most celebrated beginnings in the late-nineteenth and early-twentieth century, to its transformations and its dissipation after the renewed horror of WWII. Through close textual analysis and careful consideration of critical and theoretical developments during this period, this course will map the ways in which modernist writers broke with the traditions they felt they would otherwise have inherited. It will also show how the politics of literature is manifested in the formal innovations associated with these writers.

5ChaucerLEO006P05
Geoffrey Chaucer is the most widely studied medieval English writer. His works, composed in the second half of the fourteenth century, reflect and inflect existing English and European literary traditions. French, Italian, and classical influences shimmer through Chaucer’s poems, which engage with an almost encyclopaedic range of topics and interests. Chaucer’s own life is exceptionally well-documented, and his many public roles and jobs offer a window on the social, political, and literary culture of late-medieval London, England, and Europe.

8EAP: ArgumentationLEV013P05
The course focuses on argumentation and on how to write a well-developed argumentative academic essay and achieving a high level of language accuracy in both written and spoken English (C1). To develop skills in writing an argumentative academic essay, students will analyse and evaluate content knowledge in academic papers, write concise summaries, and write essays in which they develop their own position within an essay, and present that position in a coherent manner. University students often gain considerable language skills, but these are focused on academic language use. 16English Language VariationLET014B05
The English language is not a monolithic and uniform entity, but in fact is extremely varied along a number of dimensions (geographical, social, stylistic, etc.). The variation is not random; rather, it shows what William Labov has called ‘orderly heterogeneity’. The aim of this course is to uncover the order underlying the apparent chaos of linguistic variation and to understand its social and/or linguistic meanings. For this we will read widely in the relevant literature and learn to use the standard tools and software used in variationist linguistics.
This course is built around a joint research project and the content will therefore differ significantly from one year to the next. Research projects from previous years include:
- analysis of American English (performed) speech of three Australian actors (second-dialect acquisition)
- analysis of intra-individual variation in the speech of a single speaker of Scottish Standard English (social and stylistic variation)

23King Arthur, Romance, and EmpireLEO009B05
More than any other secular variety of premodern writing, romances connect the literature of the Middle Ages with that of both earlier and later periods. They blend classical myth with Celtic mystique, and oriental exotica with local concerns. Romances tell stories about King Arthur and his court, the crusades, and ancient English princes, many of which recur in the works of Shakespeare, Tennyson, or Eliot. In this course we will explore the romance tradition in England, with special attention to the origin and development of the Arthurian canon, the ideological meaning of Englishness and Britishness, the self-examination of courtly ethics and gender relations, and ideological origins of the British Empire. The course will not only examine the aristocratic culture of medieval England, but it will also demonstrate how premodern writings inform the literature of later periods.

24Kings in the NorthLEO015B05
During the fifteenth century, England was in the hands of two northern dynasties, the houses of Lancaster and York. The Lancastrians dominated much of that period, whereas the violent conflict between these two dynasties in the latter part of the century led to a period of instability and upheaval, now dubbed the Wars of the Roses.

This course will explore the literary and political writings produced for Lancastrian and Yorkist rulers during the fifteenth century. We will see how leading poets such as John Gower, Thomas Hoccleve, and John Lydgate seek to intervene in political affairs, and we will explore how Thomas Malory and William Caxton react to and reflect on war and propaganda.

25Knowledge and PowerLEL010P05
Following ‘Making Meaning: Introduction to English Literatures I’ and its examination of literary techniques and devices, this course will continue to introduce students to the study of literary interpretation, in particular the distinction between the discursive and non-fictional literature of knowledge and the imaginative and poetic literature of power: what differentiates them, and what they have in common. 36Reading Middle EnglishLEO010B05
The study of medieval English literature requires a close and complete reading of texts as well as an understanding of manuscript context and textual tradition. Reading a literary text in the original language reveals its true potential as a work of literature text with words and sentences as the primary building blocks of meaning. In this module we will therefore read and study a selection of English literary texts from the second half of the Middle Ages c. 1150–1500, with special attention to the determination of meaning by looking closely at the language. We will explore the full length and breadth of the high Middle Ages from Chaucer’s Troilus and Criseyde to John Barbour’s account of Robert de Bruce. Students will learn to read and interpret the original texts with the help of modern electronic corpora and the theory of new philological criticism.

37Saints and Sinners in Medieval EnglandLEO004B05
Religion played a very important part in the life-experience of medieval people, and works dealing with the (related) topics of sin and sanctity comprise one of the most fascinating and rich categories of surviving medieval texts. This course will look at English texts of the Middle Ages in order to discover more about notions of sanctity and sinfulness, and to witness examples of holy and unholy behaviour. Such texts provide the reader with some of the earliest windows on the experiences of real individuals, and in the Book of Margery Kempe we encounter the earliest specimen of (women?s) autobiographical writing.

41The Class Struggle in Modern FictionLEL016B05
Notions of class and class-consciousness are a persistent and seemingly dogged influence on the literary cultures of the British Isles. Sometimes deeply conservative and nostalgic in motivation, sometimes radical and iconoclastic; class is never altogether absent. Setting a literary work in time and place is perhaps no more important than its placement in relation to the dynamics of social strata and power relations. Class also gets easily tangled up in forms of identity politics: in relationships with authority and the State, with education, gender roles, and cultural tastes. This course will consider class as a foundation for the critical analysis of prose fiction from the sweeping social insights of realist novel of the nineteenth-century, to the atomized struggles of the post-modern subject in contemporary fiction.

42The Romantic RevolutionLEL017B05
The Romantic movement at the end of the eighteenth century and the beginning of the nineteenth was the last holistic intellectual revolution in the western world, with incalculable effects on thought and behaviour. In essence and in effect it marked a reaction to and a reversal of the European intellectual tradition established in the Renaissance and brought to a practical climax in the Enlightenment. Quite suddenly a group of northern European intellectuals — above all in Germany and England — decided not simply that the ideals of the Enlightenment were unachievable, but that they were completely misguided. If modernity has its origins in the seventeenth century rationalism of Descartes and Bacon, then Romanticism is also, in effect, the first ‘post-modern’ movement. Through supported study of some major writers of the Romantic period in England (1790-1830), this subject examines not just the poetic and fictional styles of the period, but the lasting changes in attitude of which they were the result: changes in attitude that have shaped the way many of us think and live today. To understand Romanticism is, in part, to understand ourselves.

43Viking Encounters in the British IslesLEO014B05
This course focuses on significant military and cultural encounters between Scandinavian raiders and settlers and the inhabitants of the British Isles during the Viking Age. We will examine a selection of Old English, early Irish, and Old Norse texts that offer different perspectives on Viking culture and its influence in Anglo-Saxon England and Ireland. Embodying socio-religious alterity, the Vikings were, if not outright feared, viewed with suspicion by their Northwest European neighbours. At the same time, however, they were also known for their achievements in literature and art.

44World Literatures in EnglishLEL019B05
This course will scrutinise the English Literary canon by looking at the texts, specifically works of prose fiction, that have traditionally been excluded from its province of dead white British men: writers originating from former colonies of the British Empire (excluding North America) in Anglophone Africa, the Caribbean, Australasia, and South Asia, but also from postcolonial diasporas. Students will engage with literary criticism and theory that has sought to understand, resist, and undo the exclusionary practices of (neo)colonialist cultural norms. Texts will range from mid-twentieth-century adoptions of the English novel form to reimagine the beginning of the colonial project in the nineteenth-century, to contemporary fictions that look to reveal the complex nexus of social, cultural, and political relations that characterise the globalised present. Such texts deal with issues ranging as widely as reactionary nationalism; faith, sectarianism, and secularism; the politics of ethnicity; cultural heritage; and relationships with the modern State, with corporate interests, and with contemporary communications technologies.
