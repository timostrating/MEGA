1Ethics and GPS TrackingHCRW30305
In this course, you will collect data from one of your fellow-students, by tracking his/her outdoor movements for a week, through GPS tracking. Also, you will collect information on outdoor activities through a diary. You will discuss the social activities undertaken by your fellow-student in an in-depth interview.
The focus is on the ethical aspects of academic research which makes use of privacy-sensitive techniques such as GPS tracking. In this course, we will move at and possibly over the boundary of what is ethically responsible in academic research. In this manner, we learn more about what is still ethically acceptable, and what it not.
You will learn through doing research, but also through exchanging ideas with your fellow students. To facilitate this, we work a lot with peer review. This gives you insight into how other students think and work, which will teach you a lot about your own ideas and approaches.

3Planningsproject AnalyseHCRW20205
The students explore a spatial planning project abroad. As a group they study a project on, for example infrastructure, water management or residential planning. The aim of the study is make the students aware of the importance of context for spatial planning.
The group formulates a research question, and is very much involved in the research design. The group selects a case study area abroad, and they collect data in that area toghether. This will help the students to learn how scientific research can be done in a specific context.

NOTA BENE: The courses Prospects of Spatial Sciences and Planningsproject Analyse are taught bi-annually for 2 cohorts of students (2nd and 3rd years) at the same time. In 2017-2018 Planningsproject Analyse is taught; In 2018-2019 Prospects is taught.

4Spatial-temporal Movement PatternsHCRW20105
Spatial information is increasingly important for understanding and solving issues in human geography and spatial planning. The use of GIS, gps and biosensor data enables collection of peoples movement and behaviour data with high precision. It enables us to better understand relationships between peoples' behaviour and their environment, movement patterns, and accessibility of places. In this course, current applications of movement data collected with activity trackers (incl. accelerometers, optical heart rate sensors, gps-devices) are discussed. Students learn how to collect and analyse spatial-temporal data themselves, and they learn to interpret and reflect on the results. It moreover is focussing on the implications, possibilities and limitations of choosing different methods of data collection (qualitative or quantitative), including the difference between self-reported response and measured response.
