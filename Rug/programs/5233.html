2MC Clinical GovernanceHCMAMC009
Despite the high international ranking on quality of medical care in the Netherlands the quality and safety of patient care are debated. Examples of failure are the high mortality rate in cardiac surgery unit of the UMC St Radboud; the death of a patient treated in separation in the psychiatric hospital AMC de Meren and recently also in Deventer; the death of a patient during surgery because of a fire affecting the operating theatre in Almelo; a dysfunctioning neurologist in Enschede and the high mortality of patients undergoing stomach surgery for treatment of obesity in Emmen. All these incidents have decreased the acceptability for medical mistakes, which has led to increased supervision and number of inspections by the “Inspectie voor de Gezondheidszorg” and the “Onderzoeksraad voor de Veiligheid”. To be able to maintain a high international ranking we will have to change the organisation of our medical care and this has led to increased awareness of the relavance of good and improved (clinical) governance. In this masterclass we will illustrate clinical governance with practical examples provided by specialists in the field. Furthermore we will explore the preferred way of conceptual thinking and its consequences for leadership development.

4MC Experiencing LeadershipHCMAMC035
People sometimes emerge as leaders because of the way they behave. They have charisma, a clear presence, or a natural gift to connect with their followers. But what about their behavior makes them a good leader?

9MC Leadership in Medical EthicsHCMAMC037
Technological developments have provided tools to deal with many consequences of premature birth and congenital anomalies. However, there are still conditions that we cannot treat and cases where treatment fails. The decision when to start and when to withhold or withdraw treatment in individual cases is very difficult. We will talk about the ethical dilemmas and learn about the different perspectives of the healthcare providers, the nurses, parents, children and other stakeholder. 10MC Leadership in WarHCMAMC006
The business of military leaders is war. But an army is not a corporation and a military leader is not a manager. Your primary task as a military leader is preparing soldiers for war and - someday, somewhere - leading them into battle. Leadership ‘under fire’ comes with crushing and awful responsibilities. You have to make decisions that can and do cost lives, to see men under your command killed and injured and to be under the most intense pressure imaginable for every minute of every day spent in combat. A clear cut prescription for military leadership does not exist, nor a prescribed style of leadership. What can be said, though, is that leadership in war is a combination of example, persuasion and compulsion, dependent on the situation. ‘The situation’ however is fluid, constantly changing, covered in the ‘fog of war’ according to the famous Prussian military theorist Carl von Clausewitz. This ‘fog’, combined with the realities of battle such as exhaustion, danger, death and fear create what Clausewitz termed friction. "Everything is very simple in war, but the simplest thing is difficult. These difficulties accumulate and produce a friction, which no man can imagine exactly who has not seen war.“

To better understand leadership in war we have to understand friction. The battlefield is a terrible place, still, as Clausewitz stresses, it’s a place we have to ‘see’ if we want to know more about how military leaders get things done - or fail trying. This masterclass will focus mainly (but not entirely) on junior leaders i.e. second lieutenants. Together we will look at many different subjects such as the relationship between the leader and his ‘followers’, you in the chain of command, how to make an estimate of the situation, how to use the five paragraph field order, the loneliness of command, the mask of command, et cetera. It is definitely not a crash course in tactics and strategy. During the last session you will be challenged to test your leadership skills.

11MC Leadership Tackling Anti-socialHCMAMC020
The topic of this master class is the tackling of low level (mostly housing-related) nuisance and criminal behaviour (also known as quality of life offences) and public disorder. In a number of meetings we will discuss the kind of leadership that is needed to tackle for example ‘neighbours from hell’. We will analyse housing-related nuisance caused by people with mental health and addiction problems. Are those ‘anti-social persons’ capable to be a leader in their own life? What kind of ‘external (paternalistic) leadership’ is needed and (legally or morally) acceptable? For example, can a mayor or private landlord oblige people to take medicines, hand over their pets or lose weight? We will also analyse how organisations (e.g. the municipality, police, housing association, mental health care agency) work together to address public disorder and incivilities. For example, what kind of leadership is needed to tackle riots? What is the role of private housing associations in the fight against rioters and low level crime (like growing cannabis and drug dealing)?

12MC Leadership within the ArtsHCMAMC042
Contemporary artists are able to thematise fundamental societal problems. They have the talent to also confront us, as participants in art, with ethical considerations related to our own role. By doing so, artists engage in an agonistic debate in a space known as the 'common'. In that common, they run in to other participants in that debate, such as national government, public opinion, European discourses etcetera.

This one day masterclass investigates how artists manifest leadership in these complex dynamics, in complex times. Both leadership as creators of art, as well as leadership as societal 'conversation-makers'. We will look at this practice from a historical, systematic and experiential way. How are leaders and leadership represented in artworks? Are architects contemporary leaders? Who else is involved in this debate? And what is really at stake?

13MC Leading the Energy transitionHCMAMC028
The course starts with discussing the main reasons why a pursuit of a more sustainable energy system based on renewables is considered crucial for the 21st century. Supported by many practical examples of both ongoing struggles towards a more sustainable energy system and of key innovations that are occurring, both the complexity of this pursuit and possible avenues for coping with this complexity are illustrated. Through discussing the notion of transitions and in its wake transition management, it is explained that common governmental and societal policy approaches (bureaucracy, market based instruments, communicative tools) all have important limits in making an impact on the desired transition. Most notably, the course will discuss that there is a lack of ownership of the energy transition of any single institution or actor. Students are to study some of the prime problems related to ownership of the energy transition and how this reflects on possibilities to ‘lead’ an energy transition and who is to be involved in taking a ‘lead’. To do so, students are to defend a specific statement on leading the energy transition.

16MC Philosophy of Political CompromiseHCMAMC033
Compromise has an ambivalent reputation: it is the hallmark of democratic decision making, yet is associated with lack of principle, deception, and self-interest. In this masterclass, we discuss a number of philosophical issues regarding political compromises. Is political compromise a necessary evil or something of value itself? What does it mean to agree to a political compromise if you have sacrificed something of worth? How to display integrity when defending political ideals during campaigns in preparation of the give and take of policy making? What are the limits of compromise? What kind of transparency does democracy require of negotiation processes? We discuss parts of the book “The Spirit of Compromise: Why Governing Demands It and Campaigning Undermines It,” by Amy Gutmann and Dennis Thompson; Each students examines a philosophical issue by focusing on one self-chosen political compromise. We discuss views and experiences with Arno Rutte (member of Dutch parliament for the Liberal Party - VVD) and Jacques Wallage (former mayor of Groningen, statesman, Dutch Labor Party - PvdA).

17MC Responsability, Value and New EconomyHCMAMC040
This Master class will not be limited to economics but focuses on new developments in our (local) society. Local development means in the traditional way, setting up business and trying to create conditions that will facilitate the creation of new business activities. This looks like traditional business in which the ultimate purpose is realizing profits, needed as a reward for entrepreneurship and needed as investment tools for the continuation of the company. Local development in the modern way means that besides economic activities social initiatives are also part of the local development and should be studied and facilitated.

In this Master class we want to develop a new perspective on local development that could contribute to local economic and social development. This perspective is based on ideas from the field of Circular Economy (see the Ted talk of Rotmans) and Weconomy (see the Ted talk of Jan Jonker). The focus will be on three elements: (1) responsibility for what will be produced and how, (2) which values and whose values are or can be involved, and (3) new ways of thinking in economics. You as participants will compare (for example by interviewing) four developers of social /new projects in terms of: which values are central, which participants are (intended to be) involved and finally what can be the role of these new projects for the local development of a region? We hope to discuss with the participants and four developers the results of the comparison.

18MC Social Influence: Power vs. RulesHCMAMC007
This master class will probably be the most fundamental of all efforts to understand leadership. Social influence is a very general term for what people do to each other. Therefore it is the most fundamental term under which other phenomena like power, leadership, innovation, regulation, conformity, or law transgress can be subsumed. In this short course it will obviously not be possible to enter into the details of all these varieties of social influence, but once one obtains a firm grasp of the fundamentals, and understands the different ways social influence is used in different types of cultures, it will, for a reasonably talented student, be feasible to derive adequate conclusions about the mechanisms of all kinds of influence processes.
Specific subjects will be, among others: human nature and social influence, influence processes and tactics in older and modern societies, the regulation of influence processes, influence and power, escalation of influence attempts, mutuality of influence, and transactional vs. charismatic leadership.

19MC Thinking about Social IssuesHCMAMC026
This Masters Honours leadership course (1ECTS) provides an opportunity to develop an interdisciplinary understanding of the social and human rights issues associated with real large development projects (e.g. mines, dams, highways, railways, major infrastructure, etc.). It will also consider how these social issues might be managed better. Enhancing benefit and creating shared value will be considered alongside mitigating negative social impacts. Ideas drawn from the fields of corporate social responsibility (CSR), human rights, social performance and social impact assessment & management will be introduced and applied to real life situations. Concepts like ‘social license to operate’, ‘social risk’, ‘human rights due diligence’, and ‘free, prior and informed consent’ (FPIC) will be introduced. Other topics discussed may include: sustainable livelihoods; ecosystem services; social inclusion, resilience and vulnerability, gendered impacts, cumulative impacts; social investment, local content and local procurement, grievance mechanisms, and other emerging ideas. Although a mining case study from a developing country will be used for teaching purposes, the concepts will be general and potentially apply to any sector in developed as well as developing country contexts. The teaching format will be a one-day, interactive training course; with assessment based on the subsequent group discussions of a case study.
