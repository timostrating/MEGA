1Bringing Science to the ProvinceHCMLL01304
In 2015, Honours Master student Dennis Metselaar did a very successful project in which he tried to change the attitude of primary school children in Eastern Groningen, their teachers and parents, towards science and higher education. Dennis noticed that children (and adults) in this region experience science as difficult, unreachable and something they should not aim for. To change this attitude, he gave guest lectures about academia and his own research at his former primary school and invited a group to visit the university of Groningen. Since this approach turned out to be very effective, several parties wanted to continue this initiative and extend it to 25 schools in Eastern Groningen. The project received funding to achieve this.

In the Spring of 2017, a group of Honours Master students has extended the original idea into a structural program that can be implemented at primary schools that already agreed upon participation.

In this Honours College project, we ask you to translate your own research into an attractive guest lecture for primary school children, present it to the participating primary schools and organize and guide a complementing visit to the University. The Scholierenacademie will provide you with several lectures to prepare you for the tasks ahead. The project will be concluded with an evaluation lecture.

In addition, we ask you to write a literature essay on one of the following subjects:
-How to involve parents in bringing science to the province
-How to popularize science among primary school students
-A subject of your own choosing relevant to the objective of the project.

4Creative Collaboration for SocietyHCMLL01804
In this Leadership Lab offered by the Faculty of Arts students will collaborate creatively in multidisciplinary teams on real life cases. During 8 weeks they will be working on a solution for a societal organisation or enterprise assisted by professional experts and academic teachers. Diversity in teams is highly valued, so students from various studies and nationalities are welcome to participate.

What will we do?
The Lab aims to develop a creative and sound advice for external organisations that will fit into their working practice. Every team will work on a case and produce a scientifically and financial sound advice or a prototype of a solution to the problem.
The teams will be formed in a first session exploring the knowledge and talents of all individuals. They will then choose the case they will be working on.
The Lab finishes off by presenting the advice or prototype to the organization and a personal reflection. But it could also be the start of a new individual project, for example a Masterwork.

This year’s cases “Cities in a changing world”
All cases will be fit for a multidisciplinary team, but they will also give students a sense of what the regional cultural field looks like and show how a Humanities approach works.

The students will be working on one of the following cases:

Museum Dr8888 and its societal role
Philanthropy and mecenat are important new potential sources of income in the cultural sector. The previous Dutch government has attempted to promote this practice by programmatically supporting the private sector to donate. An evaluation of this policy shows that private companies have indeed increased gifts after the crisis, but it is not clear whether the awareness of the possibilities has increased as well. The subject is of importance also because of the position of Leeuwarden as European Cultural Capital in 2018. Cultural institutions want to capitalise on global and local attention that event will generate, and create new, innovative and sustainable constructions for financing.
The case is to develop such a strategy for the Museum Dr8888 in Drachten.

The Oosterpoort/City Theatre: towards an inclusive audience base
De Oosterpoort and City Theatre Groningen are the largest stages for the performing arts in Groningen offering a wide variety of musical and theatre performances. While Groningen is a young city (ca. 25% of the population is under 25, mostly students) this is not reflected in the composition of the audience of De Oosterpoort and City Theatre. Moreover, the growing internationalization of the city necessitates devising strategies to reach international students and other residents with roots abroad. The project team is asked to devise a strategy to reach out to this particular demographic.

8Integration of Young AdultsHCMLL01904
Jobpoint Groningen is a labour market training center for young adults. Professionals of Jobpoint give advice and support to young adults who have difficulties with finding and maintaining a job. For some young adults the transition into the labour market is too difficult, and other forms of daily activities are more appropriate. Professionals of Jobpoint help these young adults with applying for benefits, and with trajectories to enroll in school, training, or daily activities (in Dutch: Dagbesteding).

The majority of the clients of Jobpoint have mental health problems (e.g., anxiety, behavioral problems, AD(H)D, Autism Spectrum Disorder). For them it is extra challenging to have meaningful daily activities. Due to their problems, they are less motivated and experience barriers that hinder them to be engaged in education, meaningful daily activities, or (voluntary) work. Consequently, a high number of young adults with mental health problems are inactive (e.g. do not go to school or work) and stay at home. Therefore, we want to know which successful factors and failing risks, clients of Jobpoint are facing when searching, finding and maintaining meaningful daily activities

The current assignment is to make an inventory of success and risk factors for clients of Jobpoint to find and maintain a meaningful daily activity (either school, work or special activities). This inventory will be based on a literature review and on interviews with clients and professionals.

10Leadership in Historical PerspectiveHCMLL00204
In the practicum the students will use what they learned from the lectures in the masterclass ‘Leadership in Historical Perspective’ as they further explore, in small groups, the qualities of historical leadership in their cultural and historical contexts.
The student will acquire knowledge of different leadership styles and qualities and also the knowledge of the dependency of leadership and style effectiveness on social/cultural/political context.
The ultimate result of this lab will be a collection of poster presentations depicting various leaders in their historical contexts.
This will be executed as follows:
The students will divide themselves into groups of three. Each group will select a leader from one of the historical periods discussed during the lectures from ‘Leadership in Historical Perspective I’ – this does not have to be a political leader, it can also be an industrial or scientific leader, for example.
Using primary and secondary sources, each group will examine this historical leader close up –e.g. political, social, media, personal– with the prime goal of interpreting what it was that made these leaders effective.
The results will be presented in poster format, with a short verbal presentation, and will be on exhibition in the university.
Leadership skills that will be practiced: effective communication, to transfer complex information, to practice verbal skills, decision-making, to persuade people, to motivate people, to cooperate with people, to organize, to realize the importance of understanding the context of leadership.

13MC Clinical GovernanceHCMMC01501
Despite the high international ranking on quality of medical care in the Netherlands the quality and safety of patient care are debated. Examples of failure are the high mortality rate in cardiac surgery unit of the UMC St Radboud; the death of a patient treated in separation in the psychiatric hospital AMC de Meren and recently also in Deventer; the death of a patient during surgery because of a fire affecting the operating theatre in Almelo; a dysfunctioning neurologist in Enschede and the high mortality of patients undergoing stomach surgery for treatment of obesity in Emmen. All these incidents have decreased the acceptability for medical mistakes, which has led to increased supervision and number of inspections by the “Inspectie voor de Gezondheidszorg” and the “Onderzoeksraad voor de Veiligheid”. To be able to maintain a high international ranking we will have to change the organisation of our medical care and this has led to increased awareness of the relavance of good and improved (clinical) governance. In this masterclass we will illustrate clinical governance with practical examples provided by specialists in the field. Furthermore we will explore the preferred way of conceptual thinking and its consequences for leadership development.

14MC Design Your Own Career in ScienceHCMMC00101
The students will experience what it is to show leadership, either through examples from others or by means of workshops and assignments. They are invited to construct a one hour workshop based on the course book one/two weeks prior to the course (there is a bonus involved if they take up this challenge successfully) or in the wake of the course. During the course they are challenged to test their own ideas, and further develop their general and personal insights. There will be time for questions and discussions - both with the trainers and the speaker, as well as within the group. After the actual master class students draw up a personal career plan in which they incorporate insights gained during the course, and report back on discussing this plan with an expert as well as a close acquaintance or relative. The course provides opportunities to show initiative, to plan and organize independently; it increases insights in leadership processes and personal strategies.

The central questions addressed are:
- What is important to me? Where do I position myself in five years’ time?
- What qualities and skills do I need to develop in order to achieve my personal/career goals?
- What makes an academic career successful, and what does it take to make it happen?
- What steps can I take in the near future, and do these steps differ per academic field?

This module explains the build-up of careers, primarily within the academic world. We start with exploring personal dreams and ideas, and how they can become the basis for a successful and satisfying career. We will then discuss personal skills and talents that contribute towards leadership qualities, and explore how these qualities have played a role in the career of a successful scientist. This module helps explore the route towards a subsequent PhD trajectory – the highest level of education. Within three weeks after the course students must have handed in five assignments, all of which related to the steps to take after their graduation. Their written work must be based on the course book.

22MC Leadership in WarHCMMC00401
The business of military leaders is war. But an army is not a corporation and a military leader is not a manager. Your primary task as a military leader is preparing soldiers for war and - someday, somewhere - leading them into battle. Leadership ‘under fire’ comes with crushing and awful responsibilities. You have to make decisions that can and do cost lives, to see men under your command killed and injured and to be under the most intense pressure imaginable for every minute of every day spent in combat. A clear cut prescription for military leadership does not exist, nor a prescribed style of leadership. What can be said, though, is that leadership in war is a combination of example, persuasion and compulsion, dependent on the situation. ‘The situation’ however is fluid, constantly changing, covered in the ‘fog of war’ according to the famous Prussian military theorist Carl von Clausewitz. This ‘fog’, combined with the realities of battle such as exhaustion, danger, death and fear create what Clausewitz termed friction. "Everything is very simple in war, but the simplest thing is difficult. These difficulties accumulate and produce a friction, which no man can imagine exactly who has not seen war.“

To better understand leadership in war we have to understand friction. The battlefield is a terrible place, still, as Clausewitz stresses, it’s a place we have to ‘see’ if we want to know more about how military leaders get things done - or fail trying. This masterclass will focus mainly (but not entirely) on junior leaders i.e. second lieutenants. Together we will look at many different subjects such as the relationship between the leader and his ‘followers’, you in the chain of command, how to make an estimate of the situation, how to use the five paragraph field order, the loneliness of command, the mask of command, et cetera. It is definitely not a crash course in tactics and strategy. During this course you could be challenged to show your leadership potential.

37Work on Digital Safe EventsHCMLL00504
1. Introduction: Digital Event Safety
In 2018, Leeuwarden is the European Capital of Culture and the city has a large number of events . They must be well organized and therefore also safe because the city presents itself to the country, Europe and the world. The mayor supervises the security of the events (Article 174 paragraph 1 Gw). As far as known, 'digital security' is not yet an issue in judging the safety of an event. Any event where networked digital devices plays a role in the movement of motion, data storage, image or sound, poses a digital security risk.

Security of events is a matter for now and the future and therefore good foundation must be laid. Practical research may help. Questions to investigate are:
1. What threats can endanger the digital security of an event?
2. What requirements regarding digital security can the mayor reasonably put to the organizer of an event?
3. Should compliance with those requirements be verified? If so, by who and how?
4. What can the mayor do when he receives information that a system of an event is threatened?
5. What must or can the mayor do when a system is successfully attacked digitally?
These questions focus on different links of the security chain, especially proactivity, prevention, preparation and repression. The research is benefited from a multidisciplinary approach, because the subject not only asks for issues of administrative law (eg, what is reasonable, who is responsible) but also technical (eg what is possible, where are weaknesses ) And criminological (eg how do attackers/hackers work, what is punishable). The result of the research is a first version of a 'Guide Digital Safe Events', possibly in the form of an app. The guide will be future oriented and so designed that it can be easily developed to a next stage.
