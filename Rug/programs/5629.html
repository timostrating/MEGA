3Arabic IUCGWL103
World Language Program: Arabic (Year 1) is a course in Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) for beginners. MSA is the language of formal communication in the entire Arab World, spanning a region from Morocco in the West to Iraq in the East. It is the language of literature, politics, religion, and journalism throughout this region, as well as a great starting point for learning the national dialects associated with it. The course aims to introduce the students to the basics of MSA, and to provide the tools necessary to build on those basics through self-study. Equal attention will be given to the skills of understanding, reading, writing and speaking the language. Additionally, the students will be offered a glimpse into a few aspects of this ethnically, religiously and culturally diverse region such as custom, etiquette, religion, and history.

5ArtivismUCGHU110
This course examines how art and activism come together in so-called artivism. We will work around the concept of artistic citizenship to investigate questions about the responsibility of the artist in a civic society and how artists give expression to their artistic citizenship. We will depart from fundamental questions about the value of the arts in society and whether the arts should and can be put to work to strive for a more inclusive and just society; to address issues like migration, human rights or discrimination. What role can the arts fulfil in activism, by which means, for which audiences and with what effects?

During the course the group will compile a portfolio with portraits of artivists in which we explore how artists working in different geographic areas of the world, use their art as a medium for their activism, or how activists use art as a means to pursue their goals. We will look for examples of artivists working with visual or performing arts, using documentary or literature, or are urban or digital artivists. We will reflect on their stories, motivations, work, and the the impact they have or would like to achieve. In the process, we will be confronted with the praxis of artivists and think about the implications for the education of artists.

6Astronomy for PoetsUCGSC108
As the title suggests, this is a course about Astronomy for non-science majors, introducing them to the science behind the images. Many different Astronomical objects are used as a component in a work of Art, and several Astronomical events have had impact on history. Students in non-science majors are often not aware of the nature of the relevant astronomical objects. In this course we will investigate the art works, historical evens and the astronomical objects associated with them. Some examples are the depiction of Halley’s comet on the Bayeux Tapestry (~1070 AD), the supernova petroglyph in Chaco Canyon, NM (1054) and “The Planets: Suite for small orchestra” by Gustav Holst (1916).

In addition to such artistic impressions, we will discuss the “Astronomy Picture of the Day” from NASA, and thus cover some more contemporary astronomical observations.

7Atlas of City StoriesUCGHU209
What are the stories of the city, and what do they tell us about the broader context of the times in which we live? Centering on the inner city of Groningen, these are the two main questions we will ask in the collaborative course Atlas of City Stories. In this course students from Minerva and University College Groningen will focus on uncovering and representing narratives of city residents, and bringing them into relation to broader systemic contexts. Groningen is rapidly becoming more international and the city centre is changing to reflect this. We will work with concepts such as urbanism, cosmopolitanism, the public domain, network society, cultural mobility and social cohesion to create layered, multimedia portraits of residents in their broader urban context.

The overarching focus of this course is methodical: we will be investigating the notion of 'science as correspondence' (Ingold), where there is mutual responsiveness between researcher and research 'object'. How does this kind of research: observing and interacting with your physical environment, interviewing residents, compiling narratives, representing visually, function as a means of gaining knowledge and how does it contrast with what we usually think of as science?

9BiochemistryUCGSC109
Biochemistry is the study of living organisms at the molecular level. This course focuses on the structure and biochemical properties of the biological macromolecules, as well as the fundamental biochemical processes in living cells. The course starts with an explanation of the unity of life and other key concepts. Subsequently, the structure, function and biochemical properties of three biological macromolecules (proteins, nucleic acids and carbohydrates) are discussed in detail. Special attention is given to the enzymes, a very important group of proteins.

Experiments used in biochemical research show how all this knowledge can be applied. Biochemistry affects every aspect of our world. Therefore, biological and clinical examples will be used to show how essential biochemistry is to everyday life.

11Calculus 2UCGAC202A
Calculus 2 is one of the modules of the Research and Methodology: Year 2 course. It is aimed at those who are oriented towards major in Physics, Health and Life Science and Economics.

The course Calculus 2 gives a classical introduction to the field of multivariable calculus. The course proceeds the course Calculus 1 which mainly concerns the calculus of functions of a single variable, and generalizes many of its concepts like continuity, differentiability and integration to the case of multivariable functions. Apart from the background established in Calculus 1 many aspects from the course Linear Algebra 1 like vectors, linear maps, matrices and inner products are heavily used in Calculus 2. In Calculus 2 the means are developed to compute the work required to displace a body along a curve through a given force field or to compute the amount of fluid flowing through a surface in a period of time from the velocity field of the fluid.
The course culminates in the study of the integral theorems by Green, Stokes and Gauss which form, e.g., the basis for formulating Maxwell’s Equations of electrodynamics. The concepts developed in Calculus 2 are used in many advanced courses in mathematics and physics.

The topics addressed in Calculus 2 are spatial curves together with their parametrization by arclength and their curvature, continuity of vector valued functions and multivariable functions, partial and directional derivatives, the linear approximation of a multivariable function, the chain rule for multivariable functions, the tangent plane of the graph of a multivariable function, extrema of multivariable functions and of multivariable functions with constraints using the method of Lagrange multipliers, multiple integrals, the Jacobian, integration of vector fields along curves and over surfaces, conservative vector fields and potential functions, the curl and divergence of vector fields, and Green’s, Stokes', and Gauss' Theorems.

19Culture CriticismUCGHU207
The cultural critic not only studies culture, like the anthropologist or humanities scholar does, but also comments upon or even expresses a normative judgment on the culture he or she is living in. Cultural critics try to make sense of their world and fellow human beings. They often do so through the relay of works of art, which they use as prisms through which they look at themselves and society. In this course we will look at the history and theory of culture criticism. How have famous critics of culture, such as Friedrich Nietzsche, Virginia Woolf, Theodor W. Adorno, Roland Barthes, Joan Didion, Fredric Jameson and Teju Cole commented on their (and our) time? What are the philosophical, ethical and/or political motivations for their criticism, and how do they legitimize the position from which they are speaking? We will also consider the ways in which works of art themselves can convey a critique of culture, for instance in the novels of Jonathan Franzen or Zadie Smith, in the films of Jean-Luc Godard and Lars von Trier, or in television series like The Wire.

The main assignment of this course consists of a critical essay contributing to the current discussions on deep history to be published on a public website. The criticism may concern a variety of topics, from the historical context of current world history to its philosophical underpinnings, its interdisciplinary approach to its political implications. 111Social facts and individual behaviourUCGSS202
Social life is full of ‘unintended consequences’, phenomena that are the result of individuals’ actions while neither being intended nor necessarily desired by those whose actions brought them about. Some examples are ethnic segregation of modern cities, unpopular social norms or intergroup conflicts. Following the classic work of Émile Durkheim, social scientists have aimed to understand these phenomena as social facts. A social fact can, even when created by humans themselves, constrain human behavior. For example, even though most individuals actively and purposefully engage in social relations (e.g., seeking each other out at conferences or on social networking sites for specific reasons), the structure of the resulting overall social network is an unintended and largely unforeseen property of the social system. Through processes of information exchange and social influence this structure in turn largely determines the opportunities people get or perceive (e.g., in terms of employment, career, housing, or partner choice), the beliefs and cognitions they come to hold (e.g., about their self-efficacy), and the desires they come to have (e.g., the desire for a paid job). Similarly, norms of reciprocity (e.g., ‘quid pro quo’) are the results of the fact that individuals repeatedly interact and are embedded in a dense network of social relations such as friendship groups, neighborhoods, or organizational departments. Norms become effective through internalization and the perceived threat of external sanctions. When effective, these norms might render everyone better off but they are simultaneously experienced as constraints: many a friend, neighbor, or colleague every now and then finds norms to be stifling and decreasing their well-being. In a similar way, social identities may arise because they satisfy innate human needs for belonging to an ingroup and distinction from outgroups, but identities may have unintended consequences such as exacerbating intergroup conflicts or leading to self-fulfilling prophecies. To give a final famous example, residential segregation of different segments of the population can frequently be understood as the unintended consequence of individual choices based on very slightly biased preferences. The resulting social configuration is nonetheless extreme: members of different ethnicities or socio-economic status groups have close to no social interaction with each other. This lack of social interaction in turn affects beliefs people hold about ingroup and outgroup members which in turn shapes behavior and may reinforce existing perceptions of group identities that emphasize distinctions between “us” and “them” . These unintended and unforeseen consequences come under different headings, such as social facts, externalities, perverse effects, or emergent properties.

In this course we will analyze social facts using the social mechanism approach from analytical sociology complemented by an emphasis on how social facts affect and are affected by human cognitions.

The course will start with introductory lectures on the micro-macro perspective on human social behavior and cognition, social facts and unintended consequences. It will then contain a number of further lectures devoted to specific realms in which unintended consequences are addressed from a micro-macro perspective. Examples are social networks, norms, segregation and social identity. These lectures will be taught by a number of guest lectures who are experts in the corresponding fields. Lectures will consist of a combination of teaching and interactive short tutorials as well as in-classroom assignments. This requires that students be familiar with the assigned literature prior to the course meeting. Each course meeting (except the first and final meetings) will consist of a 45 minutes lecture followed by 45 minutes of students presenting their course work (e.g., presenting to the group a paper they’ve read).

The problem that drives this course is the tension in Europe, which resurfaces perennially in different guises, between universal-international values and what might be called the persistent particular—member-state interests in tension with “ever closer union,” national in contrast to European identity, conflicts between national and supranational legal traditions, between public sentiment in one state and largely shared views in the rest of Europe, between business interests and against EU regulation. This module takes up one important and recurring source of tension, at the interface between ‘politics’ ; and ‘religion’ ;—with due regard for the complexity of each category. We explore in particular the relations between a still largely Christian Europe and Judaism, as these have been part of Europe’s evolving politics and ideologies since the eighteenth century.

Dutch history itself highlights these tensions. The Netherlands has for centuries been a centre of humanism and rational religion, and since the early twentieth century a primary engine of international law and universal justice. Yet during the Second World War, the long-established and well integrated Jewish minority of some 140,000 in the Netherlands suffered the highest murder rate of any western European state (nearly 80%). The Groningen region, home to a thriving Jewish community, pursued its Jewish residents more aggressively than most. The Jewish memorial south of town, the synagogue on Folkingestraat, and the Westerbork transit camp 40 km south of Groningen serve as reminders of a human capacity, in Christian Europe as elsewhere, for intolerance of those deemed different. In more recent years, attention has shifted to Muslim migrants and their integration into Europe, or the Netherlands.

After an orientation to the universal-human aspirations of the European Enlightenment, we turn first to an examination and comparison of Enlightenment-era depictions of, and interactions with Jews. We then focus on Europe’s Jewish communities and follow developments in France and Germany, because of their sizable Jewish populations and their different but intertwined political values: from the fitful moves toward Jewish Emancipation through the rise of racial biology and fusion of Darwinism with nationalism until Germany’s Third Reich. In the latter part of the course we turn to the 20th century history of the Dutch Jewish communities.

Our central problem is the tension between universal human or (pre-EU) national or now putative European values and the perceived threat of a group that seems alien. Our approach is historical: asking how different elements and strata of society, for example Christian churches and universities (including student organisations) as representatives of faith and rationality, respectively, have responded to movements toward isolation and exclusion of minorities. The aim is not to moralise or to simplify complicated situations. We seek rather to understand the possibilities of human behavior in the many and diverse places where ‘politics’; and ‘religion’ meet.

126Understanding BiasUCGHU112
Bias and discrimination are hot topics in the media these days. But what does it mean to be biased? Are you biased? Our reactions to others are in some sense always shaped by our own experience. Does this make us biased? Where does bias come from? And it is something you can, or should, get rid of?

These are questions we will address in the course Understanding Bias. We will investigate what bias is, and how it manifests in individuals, in groups, and in society at large. We will also discuss the feasibility and possible means to overcome bias. We take an interdisciplinary approach, with readings sourced from cognitive neuroscience, social psychology, sociology, cultural studies and philosophy, amongst other disciplines, and relate these to recent coverage in media and popular culture.

We will meet twice a week for seminars which include student presentations and class discussions. You are expected to participate actively in class discussion; other assessment elements are a report on one of the articles we read, and a final paper at the end of the course which integrates at least two perspectives from the readings.

127Understanding CultureUCGHU111
This course will discuss how the study of culture has a history in the field of the humanities and social science. We will explore how older historical methodologies are replaced by new approaches of research, with a focus on interdisciplinary, thus presenting a wide scope of new possibilities as well as new challenges. In the last decades the so-called "cultural turn" in the humanities has stimulated heated controversy among scholars about its methods and results. From the viewpoint of several disciplines, we will consider cultural history in both its “old” and “new” varieties, with special attention to the different academic traditions, European as well as American, and the impact of non-Western scholars on the study of culture as a global
concept.
